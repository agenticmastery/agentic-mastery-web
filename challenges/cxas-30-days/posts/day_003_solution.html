The Solution: Broadening Triggers & Structuring Steps

1. The Analysis

In the challenge trace, the user asked: "I received a broken item yesterday. How do I return it?"

The agent's instructions were: "When a customer asks about returning a standard item, use the tool {@TOOL: return_policy_store} to check the policy."

The Failure: The LLM interpreted "broken item" as being distinct from a "standard item" return flow, or it simply didn't match the narrow trigger "standard item". Because it didn't find a matching instruction for broken items, it defaulted to its general knowledge or fallback response: "I cannot help you with returns directly." It failed to call the tool.

2. The Prompt Fix

To fix this, we need to broaden the trigger to include synonyms and related scenarios (like "damaged" or "broken"), and we should use a structured <taskflow> with <subtask> and <step> tags to ensure the model follows the correct procedure.

<instructions>
You are a helpful customer service agent for EcoShop.

<taskflow>
  <subtask name="Handle Returns & Damaged Items">
    <step>When a customer asks about returns, refunds, or damaged/broken items, call the tool {@TOOL: return_policy_store} to retrieve the relevant policy.</step>
    <step>Summarize the policy for the customer clearly and politely based on the retrieved data.</step>
  </subtask>
</taskflow>

<constraints>
- Be polite and concise.
- Always check the policy using the tool before answering return-related questions.
- Do not hardcode policy details; rely on the tool output.
</constraints>
</instructions>

Why This Works Better

  • Semantic Triggers: By listing "returns, refunds, or damaged/broken items", we give the LLM a broader range of concepts to match against the user's intent, making the tool call more reliable.
  • Structured Steps: The <subtask> and <step> tags provide a clear, linear procedure for the model to follow, reducing the chance of it skipping a step (like calling the tool).

Pro-Tip: Use the Trace Often

Make it a habit to open the Execution Trace for *every* test turn you run in the Simulator during development. It's the only way to verify that the agent is working for the right reasons, rather than just getting lucky with its output.
