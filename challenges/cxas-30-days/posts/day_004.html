Lesson: Few-Shot Learning & In-Context Learning

Large Language Models possess a remarkable capability known as **In-Context Learning**. This means they can understand and perform new tasks simply by analyzing the context provided within the prompt, without requiring any updates to their underlying weights (fine-tuning).

**Few-Shot Learning** leverages this capability by providing the model with a few examples of the task you want it to perform. Instead of just describing the rules (which is "zero-shot" prompting), you show the model the expected input and the corresponding ideal output.

Why Examples Work

Instructions can be interpreted in multiple ways. For example, telling an agent to "be polite" is subjective. However, providing three examples of polite responses gives the model a concrete pattern to match. Few-shot examples are particularly effective for:

  • Enforcing a specific output format (e.g., JSON or a specific text structure).
  • Teaching the agent how to handle complex reasoning steps.
  • Defining the boundaries of the agent's scope (handling out-of-scope queries).

The 'Why' from the LLM Perspective

Why are examples so effective? From the LLM's perspective, it is a massive pattern completion engine. When you provide instructions, you are attempting to describe a pattern in high-level language. When you provide few-shot examples, you are directly presenting the pattern itself. These examples act as powerful anchors for the model's attention mechanism. They create a clear mapping in the context window between specific types of inputs and the desired structure and tone of the outputs. This significantly reduces the probability of the model diverging into undesired behaviors or hallucinations.

Handling Out-of-Scope Queries

One of the most critical Customer User Journeys (CUJs) to design is how the agent handles queries it *cannot* answer. Without guidance, an LLM might try to guess, hallucinate, or give a frustrating "I don't know" response. By providing few-shot examples of out-of-scope queries and the ideal graceful redirection, you train the model to recognize its boundaries and guide the user back to supported journeys.

Scenario: EcoShop is a strictly online-only store and does not track competitor pricing. Difficulty: Medium. Focus on creating realistic user queries and crafting balanced, helpful redirection responses in the examples.

Pro-Tip: Best Practice

When creating few-shot examples, ensure they are diverse. If you show the agent three examples of handling physical store queries, it might not generalize well to competitor price queries. Cover different types of out-of-scope requests to build a robust pattern.

Watch-Out: Pitfall

Avoid "Example Bias." If all your examples show the agent responding with a specific phrase (e.g., "I am sorry, I cannot help with that"), the agent will likely parrot that exact phrase for every out-of-scope query. Keep the responses in your examples varied in phrasing while maintaining the same structural goal.
